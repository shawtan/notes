Economics

Economic Sectors

  1. Primary
    • Harvest or extraction
    • e.g Agriculture, fishing, forestry, mining
  2. Secondary
    • Value added
    • Manufacturing, processing, construction
  3. Tertiary
    • Service
    • e.g Tourism
  4. Quaternary
    • White-collar / executive services
    • Information
    • Highly specialized
    • Spatially independent
  5. Transportation and utilities

Spatial Aspects

  • Strong ties: primary and some secondary
    • Resource location
    • Labour moves to where resources are
  • Weak: secondary, tertiary, quaternary
    • Locate near the customer
    • Manufacturing Factors of production
      • Direct: raw materials, labour, markets, energy
      • Indirect: technology, transportation, infrastructure, financial system, taxes
      • e.g Aluminum smelting is in Kitimat, BC far from extracting site in Australia because
        • there is cheap hydro and
        • a deep water port for transportation
  • Transportation modes
    • Water, rail, road, air, pipeline
  • Least-cost theory: point at which total of all costs associated with industry are minimized
    • Use computer models to calculate the best location
    • e.g auto plants are located in north east USA
  • Location interdependence theory: industries locate near each other
    • AKA agglomeration
    • Location monopolies
    • Equal market access
    • Mutual advantage from nearby industries
    • e.g Silicon Valley
      • Skilled labour + innovative technology + venture capital + reliable infrastructure + locational interdependence
    • e.g TO Entertainment district

Global production

  • Outsourcing: sub-contracting work to arms-length/independent companies
    • Cost reduction
    • Flexibility in production
    • Local or foreign
  • Offshoring: moving operations from high to low cost countries

Post-Industrial Economies

  • Economies with rapidly declining primary & secondary economies
  • Focused on tertiary and higher sectors
    • e.g Financial services, IT, consulting, data mining

Human Impacts on Environment

  • Humans greatly modify and impact the environment
    • Resource consumption
    • Waste production
    • Habitat modification to produce food

Nature as a Concept

  • Society shapes and controls nature
  • Global leaders had many conferences discussing environmental protection and sustainable development, but no concrete agreements
  • Human population is growing, and resource consumption is higher
  • Positive changes
    • National ministries to monitor environmental improvements
    • Phasing out leaded gasoline, DDT, ozone-depleting chemicals
    • Growing public interest in the environment
    • More concern for sustainability
  • Technology is not a solution to environmental problems

Nature and Society Defined

  • Nature: physical universe + social creation
    • Reflection of society
  • Society: sum of inventions, institutions, relationships created and reproduced by human beings across places and times
  • Society shapes people’s understanding and uses of nature
  • Nature shapes society
  • Technology mediates the nature-society relationship
  • Technology:
    • physical objects or artifacts (e.g the plow)
    • activities or processes (e.g steel-making to produce a plow)
    • knowledge or know-how (e.g plow-based agriculture)
  • Human Impact: any change (positive or negative) caused by the actions of humans
    • Can be direct or indirect
    • e.g Hoa Binh dam in Vietnam
      • Direct impact: water reservoir
      • Indirect impact: increased sediment in water, making it pink
  • Quantifying human impact
    1. Ecological footprint: measure of biologically productive land area (biocapacity) required to support a given population by providing for its needs and absorbing its wastes
      • Includes resources used from all over the globe
      • Helps visualize growth and scale of humans on Earth
      • Global average: 2.84 hectares per capita
      • Earth biocapacity: 1.73 hectares per capita
      • Canada: 8.17 hectares per capita
    2. I = PAT
      • Impact on Earth’s resources = Population * Affluence (per capita income) * Technology (factor)
      • Relates human pressure on environment to level of affluence and technology
      • Population: less people means less pressure on resources
        • But more people means greater labour force
      • Affluence: high affluence is a drain on resources
        • Core countries with high affluence are more effective in protecting their environments
          • However they do so by exporting waste elsewhere
          • But that increases affluence of developing countries
      • Technology:
        • Affects environment by
          • harvesting of resources
          • emission of wastes in manufacture of goods and services
          • emission of waste in the consumption of goods and services
        • Technological innovation shifts demand of resources
        • Technology can be a solution or a problem
          • e.g Nuclear is cleaner than fossil fuels
      • All these factors have complex effects on the environment

Nature-Society Interactions

  • Cultural ecology: study of how human society adapted to environmental challenges
    • e.g Irrigation for arid soil, terracing for steep slopes
    • e.g Agricultural chemicals
  • Humans modify environments and landscapes
  • Can result in environmental degradation or pollution
  • “Social production of nature”: refashioning of landscapes and species by human activity (e.g capitalist production and labour processes)
  • Fossil fuels + land use cause global warming
    • Results in more extreme weather
    • Political aspect: lower income people are more at risk
    • Core countries produce carbon dioxide emissions
    • Peripheral countries burn coal and deforest environment
  • Political ecology: relating patterns of resource use to political and economic factors

Agrochemicals: DDT

  • Used as an insecticide to control malaria and typhus
  • Little understanding of toxicity or bioconcentration
  • Book “Silent Spring” written by Rachel Carson
  • DDT destroys bird egg shell
  • Dying bald eagles made americans ban DDT

Human Action and Recent Environmental Change

  • Industrialization had the biggest impact on the environment
  • Also urbanization
  • Anthropocene: modern geological era where humans have dramatically affected the global environment
    • Disagreement of whether this began with Industrial Revolution or rise of agriculture

Impact of Energy Needs

  • Most of world population relies on nonrenewable energy resources

Fossil Fuels

  • Proportions of world’s current supply of primary energy:
    • 32% oil
      • Produced in Middle East
    • 29% coal
      • Produced in China
    • 21% natural gas
    • …
    • 5% nuclear
      • Core countries
    • 2% hydro
    • 1% renewable sources
  • Energy consumption increasing
    • Affluent core regions have higher energy consumption
  • Every stage of energy conversion impacts nature
    • Mining depletes vegetation and topsoil
    • Burning things releases harmful chemicals
      • Natural gas is relatively clean
  • Hydraulic fracturing (fracking): injecting pressurized mix of water, sand, chemicals into bedrock to create artificial fractures
    • Fractures allow oil and gas to flow into the borehole and be extracted
    • Uses lots of water and toxic chemicals

Nuclear Energy

  • Cleaner and more efficient than fossil fuels
  • Safety concerns of meltdowns (e.g Chernobyl, Fukushima)
  • Some countries banned nuclear energy
  • Some countries building new nuclear plants
  • Problem of how to dispose nuclear waste

Biomass Fuels

  • Periphery countries rely on biomass (wood, animal dung) for energy
  • Air pollution from wood burning
  • Easier to extract than oil or gas
  • Deforestation to get firewood

Hydropower

  • Dams change the ecosystem
    • Changes aquatic ecosystem, downstream flow, etc
  • Changes surrounding ecosystem due to weight of water
  • Drowned vegetation decompose and produces methyl mercury which bioaccumulates
  • Waters incubate mosquitoes that carry disease
  • Dam projects usually on aboriginal lands

Energy-Related Pollutants

  • Hydro produces less pollutants
  • Coal, gas, factories, cars cause atmosphere pollution
  • Causes acid rain
    • Europe is recovering due to stronger environmental regulations
  • China has the world’s worst air pollution
    • Asian Brown Cloud of pollutants hovers over Asia during the winter
    • Reduces solar radiation reaching the Earth’s surface, reducing plant productivity and trapping heat

Alternative Energy Sources

  • Solar, wind, geothermal, tidal energy is clean
  • Wind is super successful
  • Solar: China’s been flooding the global market with solar panels
  • We are reducing energy consumption with more efficient appliances

Impact of Land-Use Change

  • Land is cleared for fuel, farming, grazing, resource extraction, highways, energy generation
  • Land categories:
    • Forest
    • Cultivated land
    • Grassland
    • Wetland
    • Areas of settlement
  • Land changes through:
    • Conversion: wholesale transformation of land from one to another
    • Modification: altering of existing land use
  • Lots of forest land has been changed

Forest

  • Deforestation: permanent clearing and destruction of forests
  • Loss of biodiversity
  • Destabilizes oxygen and carbon dioxide cycles of the forests
  • Peripheral countries cut forests to expand economy
    • Hardwood logging, livestock land, cocoa production
  • Forestry industry clear cuts forests to produce lumber and paper
  • Core countries replant forests, farmers let forest take over
  • Peripheral countries have accelerated forest clearing

Cultivated Lands

  • Expansion of croplands since 1700
  • Croplands in core region now constant or decreasing
  • Peripheral countries have expanding cropland
  • Growing population and outsourcing of food production to peripheral countries
  • “Land grabs”: corporation and countries purchasing agricultural land in other countries
    • Guarantee of future food supplies

Grasslands

  • Used as pasture for livestock
  • Human impacts:
    1. Conversion of grassland for other uses (settlement)
    2. Degradation from overgrazing
      • Desertification: spread of dessert conditions due to deforestation, overgrazing, poor agricultural practices
      • e.g Grasslands bordering Sahara Desert

Wetlands

  • Swamps, marshes, bogs, peatlands, shores of bodies of water
  • Draining/filling wetlands and converting to settlement or cultivation
    • e.g San Francisco converted wetlands near the bay to expand downtown
  • Modification of wetlands changes water quality and ecosystems

Globalization of Environment

  • Global change: combination of political, economic, social, historical, environmental problems that all humans need to deal with

Environmental Sustainability

  • Most environmental threats are in peripheral countries
  • Environmental pollution and degradation will cause a catastrophe
  • Peripheral countries have less money to cope with threats
  • Sustainable development: obligation to preserve resources and landscapes for future generations
    • Ecological, economic, social measures to prevent environment degradation but still have economic growth and social equality
    • Impacts on environment should be benign or manageable

Marine Environments

  • 5 main threats to reefs
    1. Overexploitation
      • Fishing and unsustainable use
      • Anchor damage
      • Bomb fishing
      • Cyanide fishing
    2. Coastal development
      • Tourism and recreation
      • Dredging
      • Construction materials
      • Building on reefs
      • Sewage
      • Sediments
    3. Inland pollution and erosion
      • Soil and sediment from deforestation and inappropriate farming practices
      • Plastics / other litter
        • North pacific gyre (trash vortex)
        • Microplastics are bad

          • 85% of plastic in ocean is microplastics

          • Cannot be cleaned up by shoreline cleanups
        • Trash water wheel used in Baltimore
        • Coraball: kickstarter for thing in laundry that catches plastics
      • Non-point source pollution: millions of points of origin
    4. Marine pollution
      • Oil spills
      • Debris
        • Shipping containers: 10000/yr
    5. Climate Change-related Ocean warming
      • Coral bleaching
      • High water temperature triggers coral bleaching, which kills the coral
