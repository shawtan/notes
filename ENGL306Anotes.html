Syntax

  • Study of how we put words together in sentences

Syntax and Grammar

  • Words need to be arranged in an order
  • Sentences can be non-nonsensical, but grammatical (e.g Jabberwocky)

Syntactic rules

  • Rules by which we combine words into sentences
  • Descriptive, not prescriptive (what people do, not what people should do)
  • Rules about combining different classes of words
  • Language speakers just ‘know’ these rules, and ‘know’ the vocabulary (where each word can be used)
    • e.g ‘kills’ is a verb
  • Violations of a rule results in incorrect sentences
    • e.g “Kills Joe” needs a subject
    • e.g “Joe without needs Sam”

Sentence

  • Sentences are not strings of words
  • Sentences are composed of discrete combinations of words, which stick together
    • e.g (The mathematics student) found (the crates of books (with torn pages)) (in the hallway)
  • Hierarchical: rules tell us how to build progressively larger sentences
    • e.g “Barry looked up Ellen’s phone number” vs “Barry looked up Ellen’s nose”
      • Barry (looked up) Ellen’s phone number
      • Barry (looked) (up Ellen’s nose)
  • The word groupings determines how words can be moved around
  • Constituent Sentences

Constituency Tests

  • Transposition: moving phrases around
    • e.g “The mathematics student (put) (the crates of books with torn pages) (in the hallway).”
      • Incorrect: “(The crates of books with torn pages) (in the hallway) were (put) by the mathematics student.”
      • Correct: “The crates of books with torn pages were (put) (in the hallway) by the mathematics student.”
        • “in the hallway” needs to be with “put”
  • Fragments: rephrase sentence as question and answer
    • e.g “The mathematics student put the crates of books with torn pages in the hallway.”
      • Incorrect: “What did the mathematics student put? The crates of books with torn pages in the hallway.”
      • Correct: “What did the mathematics student put in the hallway? The crates of books with torn pages.”
  • Substitution: replace noun phrases with ‘one’/‘ones’/‘them’/‘it’, and verb phrases with ‘did’/‘did so’
    • e.g “The mathematics student put the crates of books with torn pages in the hallway.”
      • Incorrect: “The mathematics student put the crates of books with torn pages in the hallway and the ones with good pages on the bookshelf.”
      • Correct: “The mathematics student put the crates of books with torn pages in the hallway and the crates of ones with good pages on the bookshelf.”

Syntactic Analysis

  • Determine which parts of the sentence form a structure with other parts
  • Projection: phrases that extends the word class of the words its made of
    • Noun phrases, verb phrases, adjective phrases

Components of Phrases

  • Head: ‘fundamental’ word in each phrase
  • Complement: something we must add to the head
    • Only one complement
    • Cannot be moved around without the head
    • Can be a phrase
    • e.g Head: ‘found’ requires a object complement
    • e.g Prepositions have complements
  • Adjunct: something optional we add
    • Can have many adjuncts
    • Can be a phrase
    • e.g ‘Torn pages’, ‘torn’ is an adjunct
  • Specifier: word that completes the phrase

Syntactic Rules

  • ->: consists of
  • (X): X is optional
  • X*: There can be many X’s
  • { }: X or Y

Noun Phrases

  • Head is a noun
  • Complements:
    • To determine if its a complement, try substituting ‘ones’
      • Noun must be substituted with its complement
  • Adjuncts:
    • Can have adjective, propositional phrase adjuncts
    • Cannot have adverb adjuncts
  • Specifiers:
    • Determiner
  • e.g “the hallway”: has a determiner before it
  • e.g “torn pages”: ‘torn’ is an adjunct
  • e.g “the crates of books”: hard to split ‘crate’ from ‘books’
    • ‘crates’ = complement
  • ‘Dumb subjects’: Subjects that don’t actually mean anything
    • e.g it, there
The noun rule
  • NP -> (Det) (AP)* N (PP)
  • Noun phrase has:
    • Head: noun
    • (optional) Complement
    • (optional) Adjuncts: adjective phrase, prepositional phrase
    • (optional) preceded by a specifier

Adjective Phrases

  • AP -> (Deg) (Adv) A
  • Can be used attributively or predicatively
    • attribute: complement in a noun phrase
      • e.g “a beautifully wrapped present”
    • predictive: part of a verb phrase
      • e.g “the present was beautifully wrapped”
  • Can have complements and adjuncts
  • Modified by degree words
  • Modified by adverbs

Adverbial Phrases

  • AdvP -> (deg) Adv (PP)
  • Can modify anything except nouns
  • Head is an adverb
  • Takes prepositional phrases as complements
  • Adjunct: degree words

Prepositional Phrases

  • PP -> P NP
  • Head: preposition
  • Complement: noun phrase
  • Can be used as a complement in a verb phrase
  • Can be used to specify location
  • Can be used to specify an object in a verb phrase

Verb Phrase

  • VP -> (Aux) V (NP) (NP) (AdvP)* (PP)*
  • Every sentence contains a verb
  • Verb determines overall shape of sentence
  • Complement:
    • Intransitive: does not require any complements
      • e.g “She relaxed”
    • Transitive: requires a noun phrase complement
      • “She insulted (Eleanor)”
    • Prepositional: takes a prepositional phrase complement
      • “She looked (at the sky)”
    • Ditransitive: requires two complements
      • “She gave (her) (a gift)”
      • “She took (her) (to the cleaners)”
    • When the complement is a noun phrase, we call it an object
      • e.g “Patricia whacked (Eleanor)”
  • Adjunct: Prepositional phrase
    • Note: sometimes the PP is a complement
  • Identify complement vs adjunct
    • Substitute ‘did’ or ‘did so’

Constituency Test

  • Used to identify phrases using our intuitive understanding of grammar
  • Tests:
    • Passive test: put sentence in passive and see what happens
      • e.g “The crates of books with torn pages were found by the mathematics student in the hallway”
    • Substitution test:
      • Substitute did/did so for verb phrases
      • Substitute ones/them/it for noun phrases
    • Fragment test: see how the sentence is split when you ask a question
      • e.g “What did the student find in the hallway?” “The crates of books with torn pages”
    • Parallel test: make a parallel phrase
      • “The student found crates of books with torn pages and missing covers.”

Embedding and Hierarchy

  • Each phrase can stand on its own or be part of a constituent structure within a larger phrase

Syntax Trees

  • Representation of the hierarchical structure of a sentence
  • Set of claims about constituent structure
  • Interpretation/analysis of the sentence
    • e.g Interpretation
      • “They (finished their walk) (in the garden)” or “The finished (their walk in the garden)”

Terms for Tree Diagrams

  • Branches: connection between two nodes
  • Nodes: a branch point (?)
  • Immediately dominates: (verb) “Mother immediately dominates the daughter”
  • Mothers: direct-parent node
  • Daughters: child node
  • Sisters: nodes with the same mother

Constructing a Tree

  1. Identify words by word class
  2. Combine them into basic phrases
  3. Separate the sentence into subject and predicate (NP and VP)
  4. Analyze child structures until we reach word level

Examples - “The student read the book” - “((The student) (read (the book)))” - “The student read the book of poems” - “((The student) (read (the (book (of poems)))))”

N’ and X-bar Theory

  • N' is a constituent structure found between word classes and phrases
    • Represents the constituent resulting from combining a head and its complement
    • e.g “(book of poems) with torn pages”
  • N’ is a new level in a tree

Noun phrase rules

  • NP immediately dominates the specifier and a N’
  • Adjunct will be sister to an N’ and daughter to an N’
  • e.g “a strikingly hairy dog”
    • [NP [Det a] [N' [AP [Adv strikingly] [A hairy]] [N dog]]]
  • e.g “the strikingly hairy dog on sale”
    • [NP [Det the] [N' [AP [Adv strikingly] [A hairy]] [N' [N dog] [PP [P on] [N sale]]]]]
  • e.g “the strikingly hairy dog that he saw in the park”
    • [NP [Det the] [N' [AP [Adv strikingly] [A hairy]] [N' [N dog] [CP [C that] [S [N he] [VP [V saw] [PP [P in] [NP [Det the] [N park]]]]]]]]]

Distinguishing Complements and Adjuncts

  • Nouns
    1. Substitution (One-replacement)
      • If you can replace the N with “one”, its sister is an adjunct
    2. Complements are closer to the head
      • Cannot insert anything between a complement and its head
    3. Adjuncts can be conjoined
      • e.g “the book of poems with torn pages”
        • “the book of poems with torn pages and with a blue cover”
        • *“The book of poems and of short stories”
  • Verbs
    1. Substitution
      • Replace the verb with “did” or “did so”

X-bar theory

  • For each phrase, there is a ‘bar’ constituent (V’, A’, etc)
  • X’ is mother to the head and the complement, and sister to adjuncts

Complex Sentences

  • Sentences with more than one subject and predicate

Clauses

  • Clause: Syntactic unit that contains a subject and a predicate
    • Includes a verb phrase
  • Simple sentence: sentence with one clause
    • e.g “Most students enjoy long, difficult examinations”
  • Complex sentence: sentence with multiple clauses
    • e.g “(Most students enjoy examinations) (when they are extremely difficult)”
    • e.g “Most students (enjoy examinations) that (really test their knowledge)”
    • e.g “(Professors like to write examinations) when they (have nothing better to do)”
    • e.g “Exams that (last for hours and hours) (make students deliriously happy)”
  • Independent clause: Clause that stands on its own and doesn’t need anything else

Verbs

  • All clauses contain a verb phrase
  • Independent clauses must contain a finite verb
    • Marked for tense, person, number, aspect, etc. (Displays agreement)
    • In English the verb may not appear marked (e.g ‘enjoy’)

Auxiliary Verbs

  • VP -> (Aux) V (NP) (NP) (PP)*
  • Verb phrases may contain auxiliary verbs
  • e.g ‘can’, ‘has’, ‘might’, ‘is’, ‘doesn’t’
    • “He can run very fast”, “He has spoken to her about it”
  • Types
    • Modal Auxiliaries
      • Can/could, shall/should, may/might, will/would
      • Expresses possibility, ability, permission or allowance
      • Does not inflect like ordinary verbs
        • No -s suffix in the 3rd person singular
    • Aspectual Auxiliaries
      • is, have
      • Expressed ‘aspect’, if something is completed or continuous
  • Used in combination with main verbs
    • Becomes the finite verb, and the main verb is not finite
    • e.g “You have run”, “He has run”, not “He have ran”
    • Note: ‘have’ and ‘is’ are also main verbs
    • Can have multiple auxiliaries
      • e.g “He would have been a terrific Prime Minister”
      • e.g “They may have been working late”
      • In these cases, the initial verb is the true auxiliary (it is the only finite one)
        • If turned into a question, the initial verb is at the front
          • e.g “Would he have been a terrific Prime Minister?”
  • Don’t need to indicate tense on Auxilliary nodes
    • Just make I the mother of the Aux node with no sisters

Infinitive Verbs

  • Non-finite
  • Non-marked, general form of the verb
  • Accompanied by the preposition to
    • e.g “To run for President, you must be an idiot.”
    • e.g “He had to admit it was dumb.”

Participles

  • Non-finite
  • Used in conjunction with aspectual auxiliaries
  • Present participle:
    • Suffix -ing
    • e.g “He is admitting the patient now.”
    • e.g “Thinking quickly, he admitted nothing.”
  • Past participle
    • Lots of variations
    • End in -ed: ‘made’, ‘viewed’, wanted’
    • End in -en: ‘written’, ‘broken’, ‘given’
    • Use ablaut: ‘rung’, ‘swum’
      • e.g “She was defeated by a better player.”
      • e.g “He had written her a long letter.”
      • e.g “He as rung her repeatedly.”
    • Can also be used as adjectives
      • e.g “He was a broken man.”
      • e.g “His written notes were hard to read.”

Diagramming Finite Verbs

  • I node indicating inflection
    • VP complement
    • I' mother
  • Combined with sister NP node with mother IP node

Complex Sentences

  • Consists of a main clause and a subordinate clause
  • Main Clause contains a finite verb
  • Subordinate Clause
    • Can have finite or non-finite verbs
      • Don’t need to diagram finiteness of verbs in the subordinate clause
    • Does not need a noun phrase
  • Relative Clause:
    • Subclass of subordinate class where the subordinate clause modifies a head noun.
    • Usually has the conjunctions [who, which, that]
      • e.g “The man who came to dinner was very dull”
      • e.g “He wrote a book that sold ten thousand copies.”
      • e.g “We saw the film that our friend had recommended.”
  • Tree Diagram:
    • C: Complementizer
      • e.g that, to, when
        • ‘to’ is a complementizer when it precedes a verb, preposition when it precedes a noun
      • Can be skipped
    • CP: complement phrase
    • S: subordinate clause
    • IP at the top

Discourse

  • 4th level of language analysis
  • Actual speech and use of language
  • Subfields: pragmatics, stylistics, sociolinguistics, conversation analysis, politeness theory

Elements of Discourse

  • Intonation
    • Lacked by text-to-speech software
    • Indicates questions
  • Bodily movements and facial expression
  • Colloquial and idiomatic expression
  • Abbreviations (e.g Later, Whatever, LOL)
  • Stylistic features (formal and informal language, slang, technical and ordinary language, etc.)
  • Conventions of politeness, conversational norms

Émile Benveniste

  • Language takes meaning from discourse

Pronouns

  • Traditional division
    • 1st person: I/we
    • 2nd person: thou/you
    • 3rd person: he/she/they
  • They are used differently
    • I/You refer to specific people (speaker, addressee) that only make sense in discourse
    • He/she/they refers to someone else you bring up

Deixis

  • Deictic: Expressions that ‘point’ to an object
    • e.g this, that, these, those
  • Don’t mean anything outside of a context where they point to something
  • Similar: expressions of time
    • e.g now, later, tomorrow
  • Objective vs ‘instance of discourse’:
    • here : there
    • today : the very day
    • tomorrow : the following day
    • three days ago : three days before

Verbs

Finite Verbs

  • Finite verbs have tense, so they refer to events relative to the moment of discourse
  • e.g “I was going far away from here”
    • “here” refers to current location
    • “was” relative to current time

Verbal Meaning

  • Some verbs change meaning depending on first or third person
    • e.g “he swears” (description of action) vs “I swear” (pledge)
    • e.g “he promises” vs “I promise”

Jakobson’s model of language

  • Every speech even has 6 elements
    • Context
    • Message
    • Contacta
    • Code
    • Addresser
    • Addressee

Referential Function

  • Aspect of message oriented towards something in the context
  • Is true or false
  • e.g “The sky is blue”, “Ellen is running away”, “Linguistics is fascinating”

Emotive function

  • Aspect of message oriented towards feelings or attitudes of the addresser
  • Expresses outwardly something the speaker thinks/feels inwardly
  • e.g “My tooth hurts”, “I’m bored”, “That’s disgusting”

Conative function

  • Aspect of message oriented towards influencing the addressee
  • Orders, persuasive, insulting, etc.
  • e.g “Stand over there”, “You shouldn’t do that”

Phatic function

  • Aspect of message oriented towards link between addresser and addressee
  • Maintains relationships through language
  • Not much meaning, but it ‘oils the wheels of conversation’
  • e.g “Hi!”, How are you?“,”…isn’t it?“,”Who knew?"

Poetic function

  • Aspect of message oriented towards the language of the message itself
  • Literary element where forms of language are stressed
  • e.g “I have eaten / the plums that were in / the icebox / …”

Metalingual function

  • Aspect of message concerned with underlying structure or code of language itself
  • Reflecting upon language within language
  • e.g Linguistics class, grammar correction

Conversation and Politeness

Conversation: Initiating

  • There are routines used to initiate conversations
  • ‘Summons-Answer’ sequence
    • A: summons
      • Takes form of:
        • Physical movement (e.g waving, tap on shoulder)
        • Term of address (e.g “John?”, “Miss?”)
        • Courtesy phrase (e.g “Excuse me”, “Hey you”)
      • The term of address can be inserted anywhere without regard for grammar
    • B: Answers in order to continue conversation
      • Has a time limit
    • Sequence must be continued
    • Sequence can’t be repeated
    • Establishes willingness to converse
      • vs sulking, cold shoulder

Conversation: Turn-taking

  • People take turns in a conversation
  • 1 person speaks at a time
  • Ways of marking where a change of speakers (a transfer) can occur
    • Stoppage points where someone else can take a turn
  • There are rules for who can speak next at time of transfer
    • Current speaker can choose or self-selection depending on who speaks first
  • Rules for dealing with silences
    • Natural gaps between turns
    • Lapse: Substantial gap between turns
    • If the next speaker has been selected, it’s just a pause

Politeness

  • Politeness conventions are phatic (pertain to the social relationships we establish and maintain by means of speech)
  • Language and culture-specific
  • Shapes language in different ways:
    • Syntax: use of directed or indirect constructions
    • Terms like ‘please’, ‘may’, ‘thank you’
    • Naming and Address

Naming and Address

  • In many languages pronoun use varies depending on age, status, familiarity
    • e.g French ‘tu’ vs ‘vous’
    • e.g German ‘du’ vs ‘Sie’
    • e.g 你 vs 您
  • Terms of address in decreasing order of familiarity reciprocally (opposite non-reciprocally)
    • Surname + social title (e.g “Mr. Smith”)
    • Surname + professional title (e.g Professor Smith)
    • Surname (e.g Miller)
    • Title / kinterm + given name (e.g Aunt Ella, Granny Rose)
    • Kinterm (e.g Mom, Dad, Auntie)
    • Given name (e.g Robert)
    • Standard short form: (e.g Rob, Bob)
    • Nicknames (e.g Bobby)
  • Other terms of address
    • Bare Title (e.g Doctor)
    • Respect terms (e.g Sir, Ma’am)
    • Stranger generic names (e.g Bud, Mac)
    • General (e.g man, you, guys, dude, mate)
    • Insults (e.g bastard, slimeball, bitch)
    • Endearments (e.g honey, sweetie, love)
    • These are used in different situations
      • Can be used ironically
        • e.g ‘bitch’ as endearment
        • e.g ‘love’ as general term (UK)
        • e.g ‘brother’ to non-brothers

Speech Acts

  • Some verbs have different significance when used in first-person
    • e.g “I do”
    • e.g “I bet you a dollar that…”
    • e.g “I…” ‘apologize’, ‘swear’, ‘promise’, ‘approve’, ‘sentence you to’, ‘find you guilty’
    • e.g “Don’t touch that!” (warning)
    • e.g “Notice is hereby given that trespassers will be prosecuted”
  • You are actually doing the thing, not describing it as for other sentences
  • Called Performatives
  • Can succeed or fail (not true or false)
    • Circumstances need to be in place
    • Speaker needs to be qualified to commit the act
    • Needs to follow procedure or convention that is understood by participants
    • Circumstances required by the procedure must be met
      • e.g Legal requirements, age of consent
  • Failures
    • The effect has no force
    • Failure is due to external circumstances
      • Except when you make an insincere promise which you shouldn’t do
  • How to tell:
    • Performative isn’t true or false, but successful or not
    • Performative is done by saying it
    • Can add ‘hereby’
  • Other performatives
    • “That is a big puddle” (as a warning)
    • “I wouldn’t do that if I were you” (to a friend that wants to borrow your phone)
      • Is a threat
  • Ambiguities
    • “I am sorry”
      • Apology or reporting your feelings?
    • “You’re out!” (by umpire)
      • Declaring someone out, or reporting a fact
    • “I agree with you”
      • Agreeing, or describing state of mind

Force

  • Do all utterances have force?
    • Everything you say has a motive
  • Types of force
    • Illocutionary force: force that the words have, that depend on what you say
      • Explicit
      • “In saying X, I am doing Y”
      • e.g promising, stating, guessing, informing, warning
    • Perlocutionary force: achievement of certain effects by saying something
      • Effect the utterance has on others
      • “By saying X, I am doing Y”
    • e.g “I will hit you if you step over that line”
      • Illocutionary force of threatening
      • Perlocutionary force of intimidating
    • e.g “You have a wonderful laugh”
      • Illocutionary: compliment
      • Perlocutionary: flirting
